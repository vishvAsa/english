I have decided to settle in India.

Nikhil Surya Dwibhashyam, 21 Dec. 2025 ed. 31 Dec. 2025

  

## Intro
Happy winter! I plan to spend most of it in India—only seventeen more days here in this snow.

  

I’ve been doing some deeper thinking about my life recently, and unexpectedly easily resolved some long-standing questions. For example, though quitting my software engineering job last month made me seriously consider graduate school in Indology again, I’ve now given up on any dreams of acadēmīa for good, for the surprisingly simple yet entirely inescapable reason that my years of labor in a doctorate-program could come to naught in a single moment if a superior merely look up my name, find the various ([already](https://twitter.com/Saatvata/status/1661986765551333376) [existing](https://twitter.com/Saatvata/status/1998825388613530044) and future) posts “canceling” my religious–political beliefs (even if I took down my own web-site), and demand my removal from studentship or professorship. There are other reasons, e.g. the lower income and my age, but they’re all secondary.

  

This conclusion seems obvious in hind-sight, but my thinking was muddled for a while, in part due to wistfully imagining how wonderful acadēmīa would be if it were possible for me (doing something that actually interests me during work-hours instead of writing random code for corporations), and in part because this conclusion goes against the advice of _most_ of my friends and supporters since 2023, who want me to become credentialed (since my proselytism would be taken more seriously) but haven’t considered my position carefully enough. In the end I don’t regret my onymity—a religious movement led by nameless stakeless on-line persōnæ really would be the “LARP” of which we are accused, besides being even smaller—but it is true that my onymous proselytism has now single-handedly forced me to remain in a career for which I have no love.

  
### India
But more recently and surprisingly, I’ve decided that **I will settle in India in the long term** (as I initially thought when I became right-wing around 2019, though I vacillated on the question and leaned towards America in the following years); and that it would be wise for most other Vēdists to do so too, though it’s no religious obligation. I’m still open to changing my mind (not yet having taken irreversible steps to settle in India), and urgently invite good counterarguments that I haven’t addressed in the following explanation.

  
## Deracination
The key and decisive argument for me is the risk of deracination, which here entails the adoption of western/liberal _values_, an indifference towards or ignorance of the real values of one’s forefathers, a willingness to outmarry, &c. (—I don’t care about “deracination” in the sense of superficial culture like eating pasta instead of tokkuḍu-paccaḍi or listening to western classical music instead of gámaka-ridden noise). We can argue back and forth on other complex issues like œconomics and quality of life in India vs. in the west, whether immigrants have a viable long-term future in the west, whether Vēdists have sufficient religious freedom in India given its unique legal measures against cow-slaughter, sexism, casteism, &c.—all important and worth-while to discuss, as I do further below—but the most central factor for all of us to consider first should surely be how best to ensure that our sons’ values remain Vēdist, that our Vēdic revivalist effort survive more than a generation.

  

But there is not even a single contemporary example of an ethnic–religious demographic that has succeeded by and large at præserving its values over generations in a western diasporā́ without secluded intentional communities. The Amish and the Ḥasīd‑s are the two examples to which I often resorted in 2023 as evidence that Vēdism could survive in the diasporā́, but their success is impossible without their seclusion, which plays an even more important rôle than religious metaprinciples like fundamentalism; for proof one need only look to the stunning deracination of western Muḥammadans, who follow a notoriously fundamentalist religion but don’t seclude themselves, and have as a consequence genuinely come to believe (more commonly than in their home-lands) such liberal absurdities as that their faith be a “religion of peace” or abolished slavery—not to mention their deracination in behavior: their alcohol-drinking and their fornication. It seems clear that living mingled with those who practice aliën religions and life-styles universally poses a great risk to the transmission of one’s own religious values; and indeed there was incipient discussion in 2023 of forming intentional communities of Vēdists in rural America.

  

But on more careful examination it’s likely impossible for Vēdists to form secluded intentional communities in the diasporā́ anytime soon, 
1. since we’re so few and so spread out in this generation (and would be even after considerable recruitment), making it difficult to bring enough Vēdists together in a western country in the first place; 
2. since most of us must wed girls from Nonvēdist (Hindu or even rarely Nonindian) families for many generations more before any sort of endogamy can be normalized, in the mean-time necessitating recurrent cultural exchange with Nonvēdists and possibly living alongside Nonvēdist relatives; and 
3. since we’re much more tolerant of technology (e.g. the Internet, over which most Vēdist communication external or internal occurs, unavoidably for now) and have more diverse life-styles and back-grounds than the Amish or the Ḥasīd‑s. 
   
For comparison, the Amish did not coalesce from a diasporā́, but moved to America together—in an æra of much freer international migration—after they already numbered endogamous thousands with virtually identical life-styles in one small region of Europe. Forming intentional communities without an emphasis on Amish-style seclusion is a more achievable goal and could arguably combat some deracination even if not so effectively as the Amish; but even if in some years we had optimistically fifty serious Vēdists ([Rikthín‑s](../riktha/)) with the right to American residency, of whom optimistically an entire half were zealous enough to pack up everything to help settle a plot of land in the Midwest, such a community wouldn’t even nearly match the size of a typical Amish Gemeinde of forty _families_. An unsecluded intentional community of Vēdists is certainly desirable and possible in this generation, but far likelier to come about in India than in any western country, and far likelier in India thereafter to attain a population large enough to be sustainable and self-reliant, and far likelier in India therefore to be ready to begin some degree of seclusion.

  
## Cons
I can think of a couple reasonable counterarguments, which I’ve made myself in the past, to the inevitability of deracination in the diasporā́. It may be correctly objected 

1. that diasporan Vēdists without an intentional community could significantly lower the probability of their sons’ deracination through home-schooling and the careful limitation of Internet-access in childhood (stricter measures than e.g. what most Muḥammadans follow)—as indeed I’ve long intended regardless of where I settle; and 

2. that India is itself a terribly deracinated place with little resemblance to or knowledge of its Vēdist past, whether in culture or in law, and that Indian social mōrēs across all strāta (even the tiny circles of “trad” families, which—I say confidently, in part because I’ve encountered several in person—are far more modern and materialistic in the recent generations than portrayed on-line) are swiftly catching up to the west’s or in some strāta already indistinguishable therefrom, and that settling in India therefore also præsents a risk of deracination. Here one may bring up Knipe’s startling description of many-sonned Şrāutá-practicing families in India—Vēdists’ closest Hindu counterparts, some even practicing a degree of seclusion like eating only with other Şrāutá practitioners—whose rites nonetheless went extinct in a single generation, the sons all refusing the burden.

  
## Non-seclusion
But not all Vēdists plan to (or can) home-school, and even those who do usually wish to send their sons to high school or to college; and even if the obsolescence of higher education in an imminent age of artificial superintelligence render the topic moot, Vēdists cannot be completely segregated from the surrounding sociëty, e.g. since their sons surely need some social interaction outside the family, which must not be limited to the Internet but ought to happen physically with the surrounding sociëty (e.g. through extracurricular activities like sports). (I’m likely one of the least social Vēdists, yet even I say this! Other Vēdist families would likely interact with and be thus influenced by the surrounding culture even more than mine.) Thus a Vēdist cannot be indifferent to the surrounding culture, in which a major part of our sons’ social interactions must take place: So long as we not live in totally secluded communities like the Amish, a more deracinated country of residence certainly makes our sons more liable to deracination.

  
## Degree of deracination
This brings us to the second counterargument: Is America (—or substitute another western country—) overall a _more_ deracinated place in significant ways, a place whose culture is much _farther_ devolved from Vēdic (or for that matter any traditional religious) mōrēs, than India? The answer is obviously yes, and even the sharpest critics of India ought to agree. Though India is catching up to the west in deracination, it has _not yet_ caught up and will not catch up across most strāta for decades more. (And I do not delve here into the _nature_ of deracination in India, which more commonly than in the west takes place under the auspices of religion than as a rejection thereof—which is worse for religion but better on an individual level, not producing sons outright hostile to their parents’ beliefs and will.) 

The starting point of American culture, Europæan Christianity, was already so much worse than Hinduism in most ways, largely nonfundamentalist and so much more socially liberal, proclaiming the æquality of believers, the abolition of the Mosaïc law, and all the things that we call slave-morality; all the worse and the quicker then the ensuing decay from that starting point, such that the few conservative injunctions in Christianity, e.g. the clear prohibitions of homosexuality and most divorce, are now widely disregarded by Christians; and all the worse then the deracination among those who abandoned the religion altogether, the [majority](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/02/26/decline-of-christianity-in-the-us-has-slowed-may-have-leveled-off/) of the youngest generation.

  

The majority of American Christians [approve](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/02/26/religion-and-views-on-lgbtq-issues-and-abortion/) of the “marriage” of homosexuals in contradiction to their scripture, while almost half of Indian Hindu women still [find](https://dhsprogram.com/pubs/pdf/FR375/FR375.pdf) wife-beating justifiable: The difference between the two cultures is stark. 

I would (weakly) rather my sons play with even idol-worshiping Congress-supporting Hindu Indian Dalits for social interaction than with even Republican-supporting pious Christian Europæan-Americans. The former might delude my sons into some silly and easily correctable Hindu-derived hæresies at worst, e.g. a belief in false village-gods, but would view themselves as sharing much of my sons’ civilizational heritage and might not even recognize that they follow a distinct religion, and would be unlikely to delude my sons into the more fundamental errors of thought held by conservative American Christians, e.g. that women make good politicians, or that ritualism be pointless, or that caste be a meaningless concept; while the latter might try to entice them into outright apostasy, or call our Gods dæmons, and could shape my sons’ world-view more insidiously than any Hindu. (Indeed it’s possible that some of my Vēdist forefathers _did_ play with idol-worshiping Hindu Şūdrá‑s in childhood, their servants—but never with followers of an Abrahamic religion nor with atheïsts.)

  

There are, I enthusiastically concede, plenty of areas in which India is culturally inferior to the west—the lower intelligence, the concomitant lack of critical thought in favor of rote repetition, the lower conscientiousness, the dreadful grammar (in any language), the much uglier æsthetics lacking any sense of subtlety, the servile cringing personalities, the “striver” materialism, the much greater willingness to lie (which aside from religion and politics affects day-to-day matters like business-transactions)—but these traits are, I think, largely adopted from observing the parents, and it should be fairly straight-forwardly possible to prævent a son from being infected with these traits viā the surrounding culture by applying even the least bit of conscientious parenting (aliën to most parents), e.g. care towards the quality of the media to which the son is exposed and care towards conducting oneself as a worthy rôle-model to the son; and in any case all of these traits together are still not so bad as the deracination præsented by western culture.

  

Even aside from the surrounding culture, the mere fact of being geographically located in America, a land with no Vēdic heritage, likely increases the probability of deracination compared to the same in India: The mere geography likely has a psychological effect of its own. For evidence we might look to the Orthodox Jews who, it is known, statistically apostatize more often in the distant unrelated land of America than in Isrāēl (the latter comparable to India in its level of deracination), despite their circles’ (I think comparable) insularity in both. While Vēdism isn’t an inherently Indian religion in the sense of being practicable only in India or having originated there, India is its only (known) historical milieu, and the transmission of its scriptures to us was mediated through India alone; and one of the religion’s draws to many Vēdists (though it ought not to be the primary draw) is the idea of following _the original Indian religion_—the root, the fundament of our knowable heritage—of adhering to the scriptures that over all else (were supposed to) bind and define all Indian civilization; so that a son who feels quite disconnected from his Indian heritage—e.g. a son whose forefathers have not been raised in India for three or four generations, who is therefore tempted into the idea of American or Canadian “civic nationalism”, who views himself therefore as American or Canadian by éthnos and genetically Indian only by unimportant circumstance, as many do in the diasporā́ from even the second generation—is likelier to feel disconnected from any motivation to follow the Vēdic religion, and therefore to apostatize.

  
### Severity
And importantly it is not only the rate of apostasy that is bound to be higher in the west but also its _severity_: As an easily illustrable example, a rebellious daughter who totally apostatizes from Vēdism in India and integrates into the main-stream is at least highly unlikely to “marry” some random Europæan or African man, and perhaps unlikely (depending on her caste and location) even to “marry” a lower-caste at all (—I use [scare-quotes](../style/#marriage) since these would obviously not constitute real marriages), unlike in America where around 99% of the available men are foreign Şūdrá‑s. Indeed this consideration, in particular the dreadful possibility in that example, is to me by far the single most compelling argument for living in India: For the sake of avoiding that possibility alone I would abandon most other considerations.

  

Concluding the topic of deracination, I should add here that we first-generation Vēdists of the diasporā́ already tend to be excessively culturally assimilated to the west due to our upbringing, usually formerly deracinated (as in my case during adolescence), and tend to lack certain important knowledge that Indians usually have (—the only valid personal attack that has ever been raised against us by the Hindus), with some of us e.g. having to look up some basic OI vocabulary that Indians know from childhood because it continues to be used in their modern languages (which most of us don’t speak well) or taught in schools, and some of us lacking a sufficiently Indian accent to pronounce OI properly. These issues all are fixable and will be fixed, but constitute more evidence for the Vēdic benefits of raising the next generation in India, so that they not need to be refixed every generation.

  
## organizational considerations
There are also shorter-term organizational considerations: India is the only place that we Vēdists all (hailing from diverse countries) have in common—we ordinarily have little reason to travel to one another but all regularly travel to India to visit extended family, and India is the only country in which we all have the right to permanent residency—and that will continue to be the case in foreseeable future generations. While Vēdist converts have a reputation for being disproportionately diasporā́-raised, the simple majority of Vēdist converts are bound to be India-raised given India’s much larger population—indeed that may already be the case—and therefore without the right to western residency. It’s quite feasible that the fortnightly darşa-pūrṇa-māsá could be performed in the diasporā́ within my generation, requiring only four priests living in the same area; but any larger sacrifice would surely require international travel by multiple participants, India then being the sole logical destination.

  

And whether or not other Vēdists follow suit, it makes sense at least for me in particular, from the pragmatic perspective of a dē factō “leader” in Vēdic revivalism, to stay in India: to be ready to meet all Vēdists in person, to have constant access to ethnic Ā́rya‑s eligible for conversion, and to be permanently located near valuable Vēdic resources (e.g. libraries and Şrāutá-practicing Hindus). Living in India can also serve as a filtering mechanism for genuineness: For example, as some of my relatives have warned me, it reduces the risk of wedding an Indian girl who prætends to be religious just for the sake of gaining American residency through me—and conversely, the symbolism of a man who turned back to India from a comfortable life raised abroad, rejecting his parents’ embrace of materialism and luxury in favor of religion and simple living, could provide assurance to Indians, including my future Wife’s family, that my intentions are genuine.

  
## Non-reasons
None of this, of course, constitutes any concession to the [ridiculous](../okbuddysanatan/1nlzhup/) “trad” view that Vēdists be scripturally obligated to live in India and be “LARPers” if they not; and it remains a fact that many or most “criticisms” of diasporans by Indians are transparently nothing more than envious seething (e.g. [this](https://twitter.com/hbfetr/status/2002619649289277571) I saw today, “diasporans have a lower quality of life than Indians because of shootings,” which can only otherwise be the product of immense mental disability). But yes, since my living in America has been one of the more frequent targets of criticism by Hindus (many themselves diasporans) accusing Vēdists of “LARPing” or practicing a religion of convenience, my settling in India would happen to have the benefit of disproving this objection even more clearly than I already have.

  
## Backup
On the other hand I see no good reason to give up my American citizenship, and I wish for even my India-residing sons to be American citizens too. That I now wish to live in India doesn’t change the fact that Indian or Hindu nationalism is obviously incompatible with Vēdism, hæretical: that every genuine Vēdist must regard the gynocratic Şūdrá-run government of India as an inimical force, and therefore must wish for so much flexibility and immunity from its capricious laws as possible, which means owning assets (land and bank-accounts) abroad if possible—including a Nonindian citizenship that maximizes freedom of travel—and being ever ready to abandon India if conditions truly grow too hostile to be worth the advantages. (That noncitizens aren’t allowed to buy Indian land zoned as agricultural can in principle be remedied by manually searching for pasturable land that happens to be zoned as residential, or by owning land in an Indian relative’s name; I need to look further into these possibilities.) 

And while I encourage most other Vēdists to settle in India (purely for the above practical reasons, I repeat, and not because of any religious injunction), it’s actually good if a few Vēdists remain abroad, so that all our eggs not be in one basket, and so that they can continue to earn much higher incomes with which to fund Vēdist sacrifices and projects. I envision a “core” of Vēdists in India, most living together in an intentional community, who are supported and regularly visited by richer Vēdists living abroad.

  
## Cow slaughter
On the topic of sacrifices, one of the objections to living in India that I’ve raised in the past is the legislation against cow-slaughter, an indispensable component not only of advanced Şrāutá sacrifices but of rites as universal and mandatory as the Vēdic wedding (R̥V 10.85.13). But it’s not as though cow-slaughter be banned throughout India (nor is it feasible, I think, for it to be totally banned in the foreseeable future, given the Muḥammadan population): Several states allow it, and a Vēdist could live in those states or travel there for such sacrifices, and conduct them rurally in secrecy if he fear vigilantism. (There is no particular reason for Vēdists to advertise our sacrifices in the first place: We should rely so much as possible on ourselves, not on Hindus or other infidels, for funding.) 

## Secrecy
The same secrecy may be necessitated in India for various other Vēdic rites and customs—some always necessary, e.g. the procurement of black-buck skins (ŞB 1.1.1.22 &c.), and some usually optional, e.g. polygyny (ĀiB 3.23.1 &c.), minor-marriage (R̥V 8.91.5), and tax-evasion (ŞB 5.3.3.12 &c.)—but this is mostly the case in the diasporā́ as well: Secrecy is the unavoidable station of those last few men who follow natural morality in an unnatural world. 

I see no categorical difference here between India and America; and though America may hold the advantage overall, the advantage is held in certain important areas by India, where tax-evasion is not pursued with the notorious strength of the American IRS, and where Şrāutá-practicing and other traditionalist Hindus freely continue child-marriage in defiance of Indian law (as Knipe reports and I’ve even seen in person in my generation), which would never pass in America. (Here I must stress to my India-skeptic readers the importance of distinguishing between official laws and their factual enforcement: In many ways India is a freer country than America simply because of the far inferior _state-capacity_ and rule of law in the former.)

  
### Proselytism
Secrecy is not possible, however, regarding Vēdic proselytism (especially as our movement grows and attracts attention), which inherently violates various Panindian [laws](../privacy/#canary) on e.g. casteism and blasphemy. This is the area in which living in India poses the greatest genuine risk (and unprædictability given the bureaucrats’ capriciousness), for which reason single-handedly I could certainly sympathize with a Vēdist’s decision to remain in the diasporā́; and this was generally the first reason that I brought up whenever asked why I don’t live in India: America clearly wins here, having to this day the indisputably greatest freedom of speech and of religion in the world (not to mention of armed self-defense). But this reason is not decisive: Thousands of resident Indians every day utter all manner of casteist hate-speech or blasphemy on-line without legal consequence, indeed without _possible_ consequence if anonymity and measures like a good VPN be employed (which I encourage for most Vēdists interacting outside our inner circles). The Indian government doesn’t in the first place (to my knowledge) prosecute casteism in the abstract (though it legally could), focusing on (alleged) belittling speech directed at specific living Şūdrá‑s (which I’ve no particular desire to utter regardless of legality, and of which I’m not particularly likely to be falsely accused since I don’t plan to interact with Şūdrá‑s much). If the Indian government unusually decide to take interest in a casteist or blasphemous statement traceable to a Vēdist’s real identity, it might only be hoped that the aforementioned Nonindian citizenship of many Vēdists serve to shield them from the worst pœnalties of such laws.

  
## Cities
Turning to nonreligious considerations, many of the strongest reasons for which I and my friends find India physically intolerable and insectlike are on closer consideration problems not actually inherent to India but inherent to Indian (and many Nonindian) _cities_ (in which our close relatives happen to live). The [farm](../wpc/#calf) to which my aunt randomly took me in 2023 was in hind-sight one of the most important experiences of my life, for it shewed me first-hand that there are some wholesome, tolerable places in India after all. Aside from occasional travel or business-dealings, a Vēdist living off such a largely self-sufficient rural home-stead in India wouldn’t have to deal with nearly the same level of disgusting crowds, hygiene, traffic, noises, corruption, manners, roads, pollution, litter, idolatry, &c. that we normally experience when we visit India, since he wouldn’t have to leave his home often in the first place and experience Indian infrastructure. 

Unlike diasporans, he could inexpensively hire servants to deal with some of the worst parts of his country, e.g. going into town to buy supplies from the market—or the worst parts of life in general, e.g. cleaning bath-rooms. The worst annoyances for such an insulated Vēdist might then be his annoying neighbors, a fairly global problem.

  
## Income, health-care
The lower income in India should not be a decisive drawback for most Vēdists, being somewhat balanced by the lower cost of living in India especially in rural areas, all the more for one aiming to live on a home-stead providing much of his own food and electricity. India’s health-care system is surprisingly decent and quite affordable, so far as I understand from my relatives. Western citizens living in India might also work remotely for western companies with western incomes and a western work-culture, as I plan if I can find such a job: The Indian work-culture seems a worse drawback than the Indian income. 

Of course one who foremost seeks wealth, status, or comfort ought not to consider living in India, whose œconomic opportunities will always for the foreseeable future be fewer than the west’s, whose reputation and standard of living will be lower, and whose most élite colleges don’t even rank among the world’s top two hundred; but such a man ought perhaps not to pursue Vēdism in the first place, for Vēdism is the faith of the man who pursues true and æternal glory, the attainment of immortality in the Heavenly World through the fulfillment of the Three Debts—and not the false temporary glories of effeminate luxury and of honor among low men that comes at the expense of religiosity. It’s reasonable for a Vēdist to choose to live abroad for the sake of wealth, but only insofar as that wealth be intended chiefly for his Vēdic pursuits, not for his comfort, and be so impractical to attain in India that the drawbacks of living abroad be outweighed.

  
## jAti
Similar is my response to the issue of India’s comically absurd system of discrimination against caste-Indians and males in e.g. college-admissions and government-jobs, which shews no sign of diminishing and is often discussed in our dissident right circles: Vēdists in India ought to regard this simply as yet another (particularly unjust) factor contributing to their lower œconomic prospects than diasporans’, but not some catastrophic one præventing them from attending (a less highly ranked) college altogether or dooming them to sure poverty. It must also be pointed out that American colleges discriminate against Indians in admissions prolifically, Indians not being considered part of the leftist in-group; so that a caste-Indian can hardly escape the fact of systematic discrimination by fleeing his country, only perhaps its severity. I contend that many Vēdists may not even need to send their sons to college, since a degree’s monetary return on investment—generally now the sole benefit of college to us, its exclusive cultivatory benefits having long vanished and its extant status-related benefits being irrelevant to us—has been and will continue to be on the decline, and since there are ways to earn a decent income without such a heavy investment. 

This is all assuming that higher education and most employment not be (as hinted above) simply made obsolete in coming years, an assumption that I’ve only entertained here for the sake of any out-of-touch readers still skeptical of the eventual power of artificial intelligence: I for one am confident that the current world-wide system of higher education as we know it (vis-à-vis its function as a gate-keeper to easy œconomic prosperity, to competence in advanced sciences, and to credibility, its perception as an important stage of life for the bourgeoisie, &c.) will not exist by the time my sons come of age, to say nothing of the world-wide œconomic system (nor of [man-kind](https://ai-2027.com/race)); so that it is futile to decide our country of residence based on something so currently unprædictable as our sons’ career-prospects.

  
## Climate
The Indian climate is admittedly a major drawback and difficult to overcome, and will only grow worse with climate-change; and Vēdists will be outdoors more than others, whether for sacrifice or for taking care of cattle. It’s of course better in the North than in the South—less hot and humid with fewer flies—but not by very much until one reaches the mountains (the only places left now with real winters, which are not just enjoyable but spiritually beneficial), whereupon it becomes more difficult to raise many cattle. It isn’t impossible, however: I’ve seen kine thriving there during my travels, and even some farming, and most Vēdists don’t need to run anything like a large operation. I’ll probably end up settling in one of the Himālayan states; but I speak too personally here, for not all Vēdists (not even all diasporans) share my hatred of hot weather, and finding a decent place to live on the plains (or on the Eastern Gōdā́varī!) ought to be easy for some; and if several therefore gather there and refuse to move North, I may swallow my hatred and move to live among them.

  
## Air
India also near-universally suffers from a bad air-quality (even in rural areas, especially in the North, and amplified in the foot-hills), one of the foremost issues that diasporans usually bring up about India, which can be avoided indoors with air-purifiers but not outdoors—except perhaps by living in carefully chosen, extraördinarily rural and rare areas. I controversially opine that the importance of air-quality indices is somewhat overstated partly as a result of western safetyism, with many of the purported health-effects statistically unlikely and relevant mainly in old age; and that while industrial modernity has significantly worsened the air-quality, it was doubtless fairly “bad” (at least in the vicinity of villages) throughout Indian history from Vēdic times, given the common-place burning of solid fuel and the indoor sacrificial or house-hold fires: India’s air-quality at least doesn’t provide us with much _more_ of a reason to emigrate than it did our forefathers. The Indian climate does also provide one advantage: Several plant-speciēs to be used in Vēdic rituals grow naturally in India but are difficult to grow elsewhere especially in very cold climates, so that a Vēdist in India need not resort to green-houses or unreliable importation as his diasporan counterparts must.

  
## Language
One of the most major challenges for Vēdists moving to India is the language-barrier: As mentioned above, most western Vēdists are not quite fluent in an Indian language, while Indians’ Eng. is usually either nonexistent or hardly bearable; so that learning Hindī as well as (depending on the destination) a local language is requisite (for basic communication with neighbors, or strangers on the road, or officials)—in addition to OI, which every Vēdist Brāhmaṇá is religiously required to learn and to speak (ŞB 3.2.1.24), and which the Ábrāhmaṇa‑s too ought to learn. This will be quite a chore even for me, since I no longer have much of a natural interest in Indian languages other than OI, and in fact I find them ugly; but it should not be a strong consideration against moving to India, especially since Vēdists tend to be of above-average linguistic talent, and since fluency is not urgent but can be picked up over years.

  
## Skin and clothes
There are many minor factors pushing me towards India that I’ve gradually recognized over the past few years, of which I mention only a handful here. My worst physical flaw is my bad skin (despite my good hygiene) since adolescence, rather mysterious since most of my family in India don’t share it; I’ve long vaguely suspected that it result in part from some feature of the North American climate, and indeed tentatively observed that my skin seemed to improve during my trips to India. Even my weight seems mysteriously easier to regulate in India than in America, despite more [careful](../lifestyle/#diet) attention to my diæt in America. For the past five years my daily [dress](../lifestyle/#dress) in America (except in summer) has been a suit and tie with a black trench-coat, which I love and which suits me well; but I’d præfer Vēdic clothing, the simple unstitched white cloths (uttarī́ya and antarī́ya) wrapped about the body, which also happen to be healthier (for the circulation of air about the skin and of blood through the neck). Obviously these are simply impossible to wear publicly in America without inviting alarm and open ridicule, to say nothing of the weather; but all Indians know their significance, and in India (whose climates mostly make my suit unbearable in all seasons) I could get away with wearing them almost all the time even in the cities.

  
## Travel
Avoiding regular travel from the west to India and back, travel that disrupts sacrifice- and sleep-schedules and (as mentioned above) would likely be required multiple times a year for diasporans participating in the cātur-māsyá, is a major money-saver and misery-avoider (—some may recall that my most vitriolic Antiindian rants were posted from the horrible air-ports). Indian (private middle-class) bath-rooms are also, contrary to stereotypes even among Indians, better than American ones. The bidets are of course the main advantage—washing immediately after defæcation &c. is clearly necessary for ritual (or any other) purity, and Americans’ not doing so is truly disgusting and one of the few good criticisms of them spammed by on-line Hindu nationalists—but there’s also the fact that the whole room is designed to be wet and has good drainage (whereas Americans really dislike the floor getting wet). 

In any case the past few months of my life, living homeless out of my truck, were quite useful in hardening me, ridding me of whatever dependence I had on luxuries like good bath-rooms: If I’ve lived in a snowy forest maintaining good hygiene for weeks without even entering a bath-room once (as our forefathers lived), then I should be able to handle any challenges præsented by (a lack of) rural Indian facilities.

  
## Belonging
In America, where I’m often the only Indian in a building or even (during the past few months in Northern Michigan) perhaps in the whole county, I must always wonder at least subconsciously (and sometimes certainly correctly) whether an unfriendly interaction with a Europæan-American be in part the result of my race; and everything that I do as a visible ethnic minority (especially conspicuous in my case with a şíkʰā and an accent) stands under greater scrutiny, subject to likelier (even if internal, undisclosed) criticism, which could tarnish the minority’s local reputation. This feeling is a cause of many immigrants’ excellence and conscientiousness, but also uncomfortable; sometimes it’s nice to blend in, to be unexceptional, not to view oneself as a repræsentative of a demographic. In India I can be clocked as a diasporan the moment that I speak, but not before: Physically I resemble an Indian, taller than average but not remarkably, with a normal skin-color, although with less undignified mannerisms and a more upright posture; so that Indians’ eyes pass over me quicker than Americans’.

  

Of course I do not here mean to whine about racism—Europæan-Americans must be the least racist people in the world (unfortunately for them), manufactured or trivial grievances notwithstanding—but what I describe is simply a fact, such that socially and politically aware Noneuropæan immigrants can and should never feel quite at home in the west. And since Dec. 2024 a combination of legitimate issues with mass-immigration, growing [stupidity](../twitter/1994782362799149531/) on the part of the American right, and insane on-line seething from India-residing Hindu nationalists (mentally retarded enough to think that they be _helping_ Indians) has led to the somewhat deserved destruction of Indian emigrants’ (formerly actually fairly high) reputation in the west, increasingly even off-line now and somehow on both the left and the right, such that the fate of diasporan Indians may be that of a universally hated minority: a quite undesirable position even for Vēdists living on an insulated rural home-stead, whose closest neighbors would be the conservative “heritage-Americans” most likely to resent them. I don’t have high hopes for India’s long-term future, and many would place me in the camp of “black-pillers”; but India obviously isn’t a “failed state”, and its œconomic development has obviously been decent in recent years, and it seems arguable to me that whatever long-term future Indians have in the west is even from a nonreligious stand-point likely inferior in significant ways to what they could have in India.

  
##  Conclusion
Ultimately I think that I’ve addressed (internally if not here) all significant objections to my (and other Vedists’) settling in India. The only one that really worries me, to which my answer above is not yet quite satisfactory, is the lack of political freedom, on which I shall deliberate more; but the right decision for me seems clear now regardless. I conclude with an excerpt from my Twitter-thread journaling my trip to India in Mar. 2025 (from which much of this post has been self-plagiarized), when I began in earnest the reconsideration of my residence that culminated in today’s decision:

  

I’m inescapably an Indian, not so Antiindian as I sometimes affect. The land of India is where my forefathers lived for somewhere around three dozen centuries. For around a third of that time my faith reigned supreme—in the subcontinent and nowhere else in the world. Many aspects of my and other Vēdists’ life-style will inescapably be mediated through the contemporary Indian culture, e.g. the process of finding a wife.

  

I don’t hold allegiance to any modern nation or people on Earth; but if I do care about the fate of one nation, it’s obviously that whose inhabitants are biologically closest to me and of which my real people’s (Vēdic Ā́rya‑s’) genetic descendants comprise a part: Indians, not Americans. So in some ways it makes sense for me to “cast my lot” with the Indians—as much as I despise their religion and find their culture ridiculous—by residing with them.

  

Took me long enough! As usual the advice from friends and family, while helpful in giving me new aspects of the question to consider, muddled my thinking for a while; for most of my friends (_especially_ those living in India) have had only bad things to say about India and recommended that I stay in the west; but I find their reasoning essentially the same as my former reasoning: a conflation of our limited experience in (mainly urban) India with the quite different circumstance of insulated rural home-steading. I don’t like most “IQ bell-curve” memes, but it’s admittedly funny that I spent years of indecision and discussion just to arrive at the same conclusion as the left end: “Live in India to practice an Indian religion.” I don’t feel too bad about it, for even if I’d come to this conclusion earlier, I doubt it would’ve changed much about my life’s path: It would still have been a good idea to take advantage of my American upbringing by getting some years’ career-experience in America before moving.

  

Indeed I still don’t know how soon I’ll move to India: That will be determined by various currently unknowable considerations regarding the job and the Wife that the Gods give me, as will the specific area to which I move. My short-term plan is to return to America after the æquinox and to work another software engineering job, saving up money and getting my remaining American affairs in order (e.g. selling more of my [possessions](../about/possessions/)) while I finally præpare to look for land and a [Wife](../marriage/) in India.
