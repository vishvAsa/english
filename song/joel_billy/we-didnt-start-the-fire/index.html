We didnt start the fire

What's in this page?
🐎💪 32%

Billy Joel’s “We Didn’t Start the Fire” (released in 1989) is a rapid-fire, chronological history lesson spanning 40 years. Joel wrote the song after a conversation with a 21-year-old who felt that the late 1980s was an uniquely terrible, unfixable mess—prompting Joel to list the sheer chaos of his own lifetime to show that the “fire” of global turmoil has always been burning.

The references

Here is the complete, chronologically ordered list of all 119 historical terms mentioned in Billy Joel’s “We Didn’t Start the Fire”, in their exact order of appearance.

Verse 1 (1949)

  1. Harry Truman: Inaugurated for his second term as U.S. President; guided the U.S. into the Cold War.
  2. Doris Day: Rising Hollywood actress/singer who came to represent the idealized, squeaky-clean American womanhood of the era.
  3. Red China: The Communist victory under Mao Zedong in the Chinese Civil War, stoking immense Western fears of global communist dominance.
  4. Johnnie Ray: A pioneering, highly emotional singer whose theatrical style paved the way for rock and roll.
  5. South Pacific: The hit Broadway musical that directly challenged racial prejudice, sparking social discomfort.
  6. Walter Winchell: An ultra-conservative, highly influential gossip columnist and radio host who stoked anti-communist paranoia.
  7. Joe DiMaggio: Legendary New York Yankees baseball star who signed a record-breaking contract and married Marilyn Monroe.
  8. Joe McCarthy: The U.S. Senator who launched aggressive, baseless anti-communist witch hunts (“McCarthyism”).
  9. Richard Nixon: Elected to the U.S. Senate from California after a notoriously dirty, anti-communist campaign.
  10. Studebaker: A classic car manufacturer that entered financial decline, signaling structural shifts in the American auto industry.
  11. Television: Rapidly became a household staple, transforming how culture, consumerism, and news were consumed.
  12. North Korea: Backed by the USSR, it invaded South Korea in 1950, sparking the Korean War.
  13. South Korea: Backed by the UN and the U.S., its defense dragged the West into a brutal proxy war.
  14. Marilyn Monroe: Skyrocketed to fame, challenging traditional, conservative mid-century sexual norms.

Verse 2 (1951–1952)

  1. Rosenbergs: Julius and Ethel Rosenberg were executed for passing nuclear secrets to the USSR, intensifying domestic espionage paranoia.
  2. H-bomb: The United States tested the first hydrogen bomb, a weapon vastly more destructive than atomic bombs.
  3. Sugar Ray: Sugar Ray Robinson, widely regarded as the greatest boxer in history, dominated the middleweight division.
  4. Panmunjom: The border village where truce talks began to end the Korean War.
  5. Brando: Marlon Brando starred in A Streetcar Named Desire, popularizing a raw, rebellious, and highly sexualized style of acting.
  6. The King and I: The Broadway musical that explored the clash of Eastern and Western cultures.
  7. The Catcher in the Rye: J.D. Salinger’s novel about teenage alienation, which was heavily controversial and frequently banned for its profanity.
  8. Eisenhower: WWII hero Dwight D. Eisenhower was elected U.S. President on a promise to end the Korean War.
  9. Vaccine: Jonas Salk developed the polio vaccine, ending decades of terror over the paralyzing disease.
  10. England’s got a new queen: Queen Elizabeth II ascended to the throne following the death of King George VI.
  11. Marciano: Rocky Marciano defeated Jersey Joe Walcott to become the undefeated World Heavyweight Champion.
  12. Liberace: The flamboyant pianist got his own TV show, challenging rigid mid-century norms of masculinity.
  13. Santayana goodbye: Spanish-American philosopher George Santayana died, famous for the warning: “Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it.”

Verse 3 (1953–1956)

  1. Joseph Stalin: The brutal dictator of the Soviet Union died, triggering a power struggle in the Kremlin.
  2. Malenkov: Georgy Malenkov briefly succeeded Stalin before being politically outmaneuvered.
  3. Nasser: Gamal Abdel Nasser rose to power in Egypt, championing fierce Arab nationalism and anti-colonialism.
  4. Prokofiev: Renowned Russian composer Sergei Prokofiev died, famously on the exact same day as Stalin.
  5. Rockefeller: Winthrop Rockefeller divorced Barbara “Bobo” Sears in a highly publicized, record-breaking $5.5 million settlement.
  6. Campanella: Roy Campanella, the legendary Black catcher for the Brooklyn Dodgers, won his second National League MVP award.
  7. Communist Bloc: The Soviet Union solidified its military and political control over Eastern Europe.
  8. Roy Cohn: Chief Counsel to Senator McCarthy, widely feared for his aggressive, ruthless prosecutorial tactics.
  9. Juan Perón: The populist dictator of Argentina was ousted in a violent military coup.
  10. Toscanini: Arturo Toscanini, one of the greatest conductors of the century, suffered a memory lapse during a live broadcast and retired.
  11. Dacron: The synthetic polyester fiber was introduced, revolutionizing the textile industry and medical surgery.
  12. Dien Bien Phu falls: French forces were decisively defeated in Vietnam, marking the end of French Indochina and drawing the U.S. in.
  13. Rock Around the Clock: Bill Haley & His Comets released the hit song, launching the global youth-culture explosion of rock and roll.
  14. Einstein: World-renowned physicist Albert Einstein died at age 76.
  15. James Dean: The iconic “rebel without a cause” died in a high-speed car crash at age 24, cementing his status as a tragic youth symbol.
  16. Brooklyn’s got a winning team: The Brooklyn Dodgers finally won their first and only World Series before moving to Los Angeles.
  17. Davy Crockett: A massive, Disney-fueled pop-culture craze swept the country, making coonskin caps mandatory for American kids.
  18. Peter Pan: The musical was broadcast live on color television to millions of viewers.
  19. Elvis Presley: Elvis signed with RCA Victor and exploded onto the scene, bringing Black-influenced rockabilly to mainstream white youth.
  20. Disneyland: Opened its gates in California, pioneering the modern, corporate theme park.
  21. Bardot: Brigitte Bardot starred in And God Created Woman, popularizing European cinema and pushing sexual boundaries.
  22. Budapest: The Hungarian Uprising against Soviet rule was brutally crushed by Soviet tanks and troops.
  23. Alabama: The Montgomery Bus Boycott, sparked by Rosa Parks, launched the modern Civil Rights Movement.
  24. Khrushchev: Nikita Khrushchev delivered his “Secret Speech” denouncing the cult of personality surrounding Stalin.
  25. Princess Grace: American actress Grace Kelly retired from Hollywood to marry Prince Rainier III of Monaco.
  26. Peyton Place: The scandalous best-selling novel exposed the dark, sexually charged secrets of a squeaky-clean New England town.
  27. Trouble in the Suez: Egypt nationalized the Suez Canal, prompting a military invasion by Britain, France, and Israel.

Verse 4 (1957–1960)

  1. Little Rock: Nine Black students were blocked from integrating a high school, forcing President Eisenhower to send federal troops.
  2. Pasternak: Russian author Boris Pasternak was forced by the Soviet government to decline the Nobel Prize for his novel Doctor Zhivago.
  3. Mickey Mantle: The legendary New York Yankees slugger led the team to multiple championships.
  4. Kerouac: Jack Kerouac published On the Road, defining the counterculture “Beat Generation”.
  5. Sputnik: The USSR launched the first satellite, provoking deep panic that the U.S. was losing the technology race.
  6. Chou En-Lai (Zhou Enlai): The Premier of China, who consolidated communist power and managed foreign relations.
  7. Bridge on the River Kwai: The Oscar-winning film highlighted the psychological and physical horrors of WWII POW camps.
  8. Lebanon: U.S. Marines were deployed to Lebanon to secure the pro-Western government during a political crisis.
  9. Charles de Gaulle: He returned to power in France to draft a new constitution and resolve the bloody Algerian War.
  10. California baseball: The Dodgers and Giants relocated from New York to California, shifting the center of American sports westward.
  11. Starkweather homicide: Teenager Charles Starkweather committed 11 murders, shocking the nation and sparking panic about youth violence.
  12. Children of Thalidomide: A popular morning-sickness drug was found to cause horrific birth defects, leading to a global health disaster.
  13. Buddy Holly: The beloved rock pioneer died in a tragic plane crash (“The Day the Music Died”).
  14. Ben-Hur: The epic film won a record-shattering 11 Academy Awards.
  15. Space Monkey: NASA successfully launched Able and Baker, two monkeys, into space and returned them alive.
  16. Mafia: A massive police raid on a mob summit in Apalachin, NY, forced the FBI to admit a national organized crime syndicate existed.
  17. Hula Hoops: A massive toy fad swept the globe, with over 100 million sold in a single year.
  18. Castro: Fidel Castro took power in Cuba, establishing a communist regime just 90 miles off the coast of Florida.
  19. Edsel is a no-go: Ford’s highly hyped new car line was a massive commercial failure, costing the company millions.
  20. U2: A U.S. U-2 spy plane was shot down over the Soviet Union, collapsing a high-stakes peace summit.
  21. Syngman Rhee: The authoritarian President of South Korea was forced to resign after a student-led democratic uprising.
  22. Payola: A major scandal exposed record companies paying DJs (like Alan Freed) illegal bribes to play specific songs.
  23. Kennedy: John F. Kennedy defeated Richard Nixon in the 1960 presidential election.
  24. Chubby Checker: Popularized “The Twist”, triggering a global dance craze.
  25. Psycho: Alfred Hitchcock’s thriller revolutionized horror and broke cinematic taboos regarding violence and sexuality.
  26. Belgians in the Congo: The Belgian military withdrew from the Congo, leaving behind political chaos and civil war.

Verse 5 (1961–1963)

  1. Hemingway: Acclaimed author Ernest Hemingway committed suicide at age 61.
  2. Eichmann: Nazi war criminal Adolf Eichmann was captured in Argentina, tried in Israel, and hanged.
  3. Stranger in a Strange Land: Robert A. Heinlein’s sci-fi novel challenged traditional views of religion and monogamy.
  4. Dylan: Bob Dylan signed with Columbia Records, bringing protest music and poetic depth to folk and rock.
  5. Berlin: The Soviet Union constructed the Berlin Wall, physically cementing the Iron Curtain.
  6. Bay of Pigs invasion: A CIA-planned invasion of Cuba by Cuban exiles failed catastrophically.
  7. Lawrence of Arabia: The sweeping cinematic masterpiece won seven Academy Awards.
  8. British Beatlemania: The Beatles exploded in popularity in the UK, poised to conquer America and the world.
  9. Ole Miss: Riots erupted when James Meredith integrated the University of Mississippi, requiring federal troop intervention.
  10. John Glenn: Became the first American to orbit the Earth.
  11. Liston beats Patterson: Sonny Liston knocked out Floyd Patterson in the first round to win the Heavyweight Championship.
  12. Pope Paul: Pope Paul VI was elected and tasked with continuing the reforms of the Second Vatican Council.
  13. Malcolm X: The charismatic civil rights leader preached Black empowerment, challenging mainstream pacifist strategies.
  14. British Politician sex: The Profumo Affair, a sex scandal involving Britain’s War Secretary and a Soviet spy’s associate, shook the UK government.
  15. J.F.K. blown away: President John F. Kennedy was assassinated in Dallas, Texas, traumatizing the nation.

Verse 6 (1964–1989)

  1. Birth control: The FDA approved the contraceptive pill, accelerating the sexual revolution.
  2. Ho Chi Minh: The leader of North Vietnam who organized the fight against U.S. forces.
  3. Richard Nixon back again: After his political career was declared dead in 1962, Nixon staged a massive comeback and was elected president in 1968.
  4. Moonshot: Apollo 11 landed the first humans on the Moon.
  5. Woodstock: The legendary 1969 music festival became the cultural touchstone of the hippie counterculture.
  6. Watergate: A political break-in and subsequent cover-up forced Richard Nixon to resign the presidency.
  7. Punk rock: An aggressive, anti-establishment musical movement (led by bands like the Ramones and Sex Pistols) rejected mainstream rock.
  8. Begin: Menachem Begin, Prime Minister of Israel, signed the historic Camp David Peace Accords with Egypt.
  9. Reagan: Former actor Ronald Reagan was elected U.S. President, ushering in a wave of conservative politics.
  10. Palestine: Ongoing tensions and violence in the Middle East over the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.
  11. Terror on the airline: A steep rise in international aircraft hijackings by political militants.
  12. Ayatollah’s in Iran: The Islamic Revolution overthrew the Shah, installing Ayatollah Khomeini and triggering the hostage crisis.

  14. Wheel of Fortune: The game show was revamped with Pat Sajak and Vanna White, becoming a massive TV phenomenon.
  15. Sally Ride: Became the first American woman in space.
  16. Heavy metal: Hard rock bands (like Mötley Crüe and Metallica) dominated the music scene, sparking “Satanic Panic” concerns among parents.
  17. Suicide: A highly publicized rise in teen suicide rates in the mid-to-late 1980s alarmed educators and parents.
  18. Foreign debts: The U.S. national debt skyrocketed during the Reagan years, transforming the country into a debtor nation.
  19. Homeless Vets: A growing public awareness of Vietnam veterans suffering from PTSD, addiction, and homelessness.
  20. AIDS: The global outbreak of the HIV/AIDS epidemic, which devastated communities while governments initially ignored it.
  21. Crack: The devastating epidemic of crack-cocaine swept through inner cities, driving up addiction and violent crime rates.

  23. Hypodermics on the shores: Syringes and medical waste washed up on public beaches in NJ and NY, triggering environmental and health scares.

The Historical Concerns

The song lists over 110 historical events, cultural milestones, and prominent figures. Because the song functions as a timeline of Cold War anxiety, many references represent major geopolitical crises, societal panics, and direct threats to humanity.

Here are the most significant “concerns” mentioned, categorized by the anxiety they provoked:

1. The Nuclear & Cold War Threat

  • Red China (1949): The Chinese Communist Party took control of the nation. This stoked massive Western fears of a unified, global communist expansion.
  • Joe McCarthy (1950): A U.S. Senator who launched aggressive, often baseless witch hunts accusing American citizens of being communist spies, severely damaging civil liberties.
  • North Korea / South Korea (1950): The invasion of South Korea sparked a brutal war and marked the first major “proxy war” of the Cold War, raising fears of World War III.
  • Rosenbergs (1951): Julius and Ethel Rosenberg were executed for passing U.S. nuclear secrets to the Soviet Union, feeding national paranoia over internal betrayal.
  • H-bomb (1951): The development of the hydrogen bomb—vastly more powerful than atomic weapons—escalated the arms race into the realm of potential total global annihilation.
  • Sputnik (1957): The USSR successfully launched the first artificial satellite. It terrified the West, signaling that the Soviets had developed superior rocket technology capable of launching nuclear missiles across continents.
  • U2 (1960): A U.S. spy plane was shot down over Soviet airspace, instantly freezing a period of easing diplomatic relations between Eisenhower and Khrushchev.
  • Berlin (1961): The construction of the Berlin Wall physicalized the divide of Europe, separating families and creating an incredibly tense, military-guarded flashpoint.
  • Bay of Pigs invasion (1961): A failed CIA-backed attempt to overthrow Fidel Castro in Cuba. It deeply embarrassed the U.S. and ultimately led to the Cuban Missile Crisis.

2. Major Geopolitical & Regional Flashpoints

  • Budapest (1956): The Hungarian Revolution, where citizens rebelled against Soviet control. Soviet tanks brutally crushed the uprising, proving the USSR’s willingness to use extreme violence to preserve the Eastern Bloc.
  • Trouble in the Suez (1956): Egypt nationalized the Suez Canal, prompting a military invasion by Britain, France, and Israel. This brought the US and USSR to the brink of a direct showdown.
  • Dien Bien Phu Falls (1954): The decisive defeat of French colonial forces in Vietnam. It created the vacuum that drew the United States directly into the Vietnam War.
  • Belgians in the Congo (1960): The chaotic decolonization of the Congo led to immediate civil war and a major Cold War proxy struggle.
  • Russians in Afghanistan (1979): The Soviet invasion of Afghanistan sparked a decade-long war, destroying detente and dragging both superpowers back into fierce, active opposition.
  • Ayatollah’s in Iran (1979): The Iranian Revolution ousted a U.S.-backed monarch for a hardline Islamic theocracy, directly leading to the protracted 444-day Iran Hostage Crisis.

3. Civil Unrest, Violence, and Scandals

  • Little Rock (1957): A major battleground for civil rights where white crowds and state forces resisted school desegregation. President Eisenhower had to send federal paratroopers to escort Black students to school.
  • Ole Miss (1962): Riots broke out when James Meredith registered as the first Black student at the University of Mississippi, requiring federal marshals and military intervention.
  • Starkweather homicide (1958): Charles Starkweather went on a high-profile multi-state murder spree, shocking the American public and cementing fears about disaffected, violent youth.
  • J.F.K. blown away (1963): The assassination of President John F. Kennedy. This shattered American optimism and began an era of deep national trauma and institutional distrust.
  • British Politician sex (1963): The “Profumo Affair,” a major political scandal involving the British Secretary of State for War having an affair with a model who was also involved with a Soviet naval attaché. It compromised national security and collapsed the British government.
  • Watergate (1972-1974): The break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters and the subsequent White House cover-up. It directly caused the resignation of President Richard Nixon and permanently damaged public trust in government.
  • Bernie Goetz (1984): Vigilante Bernard Goetz shot four young Black men on a New York subway, claiming they were trying to mug him. This ignited national debates over rising urban crime rates and racial profiling.
  • China’s under martial law (1989): The brutal military crackdown on student-led pro-democracy protests in Beijing’s Tiananmen Square, demonstrating the Chinese government’s violent resistance to democratic reform.

4. Public Health & Social Crises

  • Children of Thalidomide (1958): A popular drug prescribed to pregnant women for morning sickness was discovered to cause severe limb deformities in thousands of infants, leading to strict international drug testing reforms.
  • AIDS (1980s): The outbreak of the Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome epidemic. Stigmatization and a slow federal government response allowed the virus to decimate communities worldwide before major research efforts began.
  • Crack (1980s): The devastating emergence of crack cocaine in American cities, fueling a massive rise in violent crime, addiction, and aggressive policing policies.
  • Hypodermics on the shores (1987-1988): Syringes and medical waste washed up on public beaches in New Jersey and New York, causing massive public alarm over water pollution and hazardous materials disposal.
